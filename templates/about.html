Executive Summary

In recent years, the UMD campus in College Park has seen a rise in days with excessive heat as a result of climate change. Average heat wave seasons in the United States are around 49 days longer than they were in the 1960s (EPA, 2023). In August 2023, Prince George’s County had the third highest heat-related illness complaints compared to other counties in Maryland (Office of Preparedness and Response, 2023, p. 3).

College Park has seen a significant increase in the severity of hot weather over the past 30 years. The impact College Park is facing is shown through the city’s heat index, which assesses the feel of weather based on air temperature and humidity. Health caution days are defined by the National Weather Service as days where the heat index exceeds 90℉ (Risk Factor, n.d.). Relative to the same period 30 years ago, College Park now sees 16 more health caution days per year, for a total of 58 days. Thirty years from now, research predicts that the city will experience 76 health caution days in a year (Risk Factor, n.d.).

According to Maryland’s Weekly Heat Report, complaints from heat-related illnesses primarily come from people aged 18 to 44 (Office of Preparedness and Response, 2023, p. 2). The median age of undergraduate students on campus is 21 years old, and the median age of graduate students is 26 years old (USM Institutional Research Information System, 2022). With a majority of people on UMD’s campus within the 18 to 44 age group, excessive heat is a pressing issue for the UMD population.

Excessive heat also impacts energy expenditure, outdoor activities, and campus operations. UMD’s administration is seeking guidance on how to effectively balance cost and campus well-being. After speaking with UMD administrators, we understand that the cost of shutting the campus down for 24 hours is nearly $1 million.

Project H1 involves the creation of a decision matrix that outlines specific instructions for different categories of operations at varying temperature thresholds. The matrix includes information on how UMD can best protect the health and safety of individuals on UMD’s campus during instances of excessive heat. The purpose of the project is to improve UMD’s administration’s ability to respond quickly, effectively, and knowledgeably to extremely hot weather. While the matrix will serve as a guideline for administration, outlining potential solutions and associated consequences, it is not meant to make decisions for President Pines.

Additionally, as part of the project, our team has suggested a cost-effective heat emergency preparedness kit to aid in keeping members of our campus community cool and safe despite soaring temperatures. Project H1 aims to maintain campus activities during increasingly hot days and reduce the negative impacts of heat on our community.

Continuing to create plans to prepare for environmental emergencies is a critical step towards maintaining safe and healthy college communities. With the advent of climate change and consequent environmental issues, staying ahead of harmful impacts is paramount at campuses across the United States.
